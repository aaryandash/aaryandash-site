## The constraint

Design a 3D-printed bio-filter — a Ø20 × 25 mm cylinder — that maximizes internal
contact surface area, the exact metric the competition scored filtration efficiency
on. The hard limit sat on the manufacturing side: every submission had to print on a
Bambu A1 Mini in PLA at a 0.2 mm layer height with **no supports and no adhesion
aids**, and any model that failed to print under those parameters was disqualified.
So every gain in surface area had to survive as a clean, support-free print. The
reference model — a uniform 5 mm gyroid — set the bar at 6,508 mm².

## What I tried

I started from a uniform gyroid, then graded it, packing more material near the top
and less near the bottom. To push surface area further I wanted to grade both cell
*size* and *solidity* down the height: a coarse, open top for flow and a fine, dense
base to trap contaminant. Fusion 360 can't vary gyroid cell size continuously, so I
split the filter into three stacked gyroids — 5 mm, 3 mm, and 2 mm cells — grading
solidity 30% → 45% → 60% down the stack.

## What failed

The three-gyroid stack didn't hold together. At each boundary the cells from adjacent
segments wouldn't line up, leaving broken walls where the lattices met. On a
support-free print those discontinuities are print failures — and a failed print meant
disqualification. Segmenting the geometry had traded a scoring problem for a
manufacturability one.

## What shipped

I dropped Fusion's lattice tools and generated the gyroid mathematically, from a single
continuous function grading smoothly from 5 mm / 30% at the top to 2 mm / 60% at the
base. Because it's one field rather than stitched segments, every cell aligns with its
neighbors — no discontinuities, no supports needed. The final filter measured
12,850 mm² of surface area, nearly double the reference, and printed cleanly in a
single pass. Four iterations from first gyroid to final.
